What is an airless paint sprayer?

AirlessFix · March 2026 · 8 min read · EN

An airless paint sprayer is a machine that pumps paint at very high pressure — typically between 150 and 230 bar (2,200–3,300 PSI) — and forces it through a small precision tip. The tip atomises the paint into a fine, even mist without using compressed air. This is fundamentally different from conventional spray guns, which rely on an air compressor to break the paint apart.

The result is a fast, even coat on large surfaces. A professional painter with an airless sprayer can cover what would take hours with a roller in a fraction of the time — with a more consistent finish and less fatigue.

How does airless technology work?

The core of any airless sprayer is the displacement pump. An electric motor drives a piston that draws paint from the container and forces it through a high-pressure hose to the spray gun. At the gun, the paint passes through a tiny orifice in the tip — typically between 0.011" and 0.021" in diameter.

The sudden drop in pressure as paint exits the tip causes it to atomise — breaking into millions of tiny droplets. The fan pattern (the shape of the spray) is controlled by the tip geometry. A wider fan tip creates a broad, flat spray. A narrower tip concentrates the paint into a tighter stream for detailed work.

Key principle: The paint does the work. Higher viscosity paints need higher pressure and larger tip orifices. Thinner paints need less pressure and smaller orifices. Getting this balance right is the foundation of good airless spraying.

Airless vs compressed air — what's the difference?

Conventional spray guns (HVLP — High Volume Low Pressure) use a compressor to atomise paint. They produce excellent finish quality on small surfaces like furniture or car panels, but they are slow and waste a significant amount of paint as overspray.

Airless sprayers sacrifice some finish refinement in exchange for speed and coverage. On walls, ceilings, fences, floors, and large structural surfaces, airless is the professional's choice. The overspray is lower than HVLP at distance, and coverage rates are dramatically higher.

What paints can you use?

Most water-based and oil-based paints can be sprayed airless. The key variable is viscosity — how thick the paint is. Thicker paints require more pressure and larger tip orifices. Thinner paints need less pressure and smaller tips.

  • Wall emulsions and interior paints — the easiest to spray, suitable for all models
  • Exterior masonry paints — thicker, require A45 or above, larger tip
  • Primers and undercoats — often thick, A60 or above recommended
  • Lacquers and varnishes — thin, spray easily, good finish achievable with A30+
  • Roof coatings and elastomeric paints — very thick, A80 or A100 required
  • Fire retardant coatings — A100 only, specific tips required

Never spray materials with flash points below 38°C (100°F) — this includes many solvent-based products. Always check the paint manufacturer's SDS before spraying.

How long do airless sprayers last?

A well-maintained machine can last 10–15 years in regular professional use. The components that wear first are the displacement pump packings, the inlet and outlet valves, and the spray tip. These are all serviceable parts available as spare kits.

The single most important maintenance step is proper winterisation — flushing with water or mineral spirits and adding Throat Seal Liquid (TSL) to protect the pump seals during storage. Machines that are stored wet or frozen rarely survive more than two or three seasons.
